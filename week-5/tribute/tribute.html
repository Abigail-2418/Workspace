Martin Luther King

"Darkness cannot drive out darkness: only light can do that. Hate cannot drive out hate: only love can do that."

- Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.

1929

Martin Luther King Jr. was born on January 15, 1929, in Atlanta, Georgia. Growing up as the son of a pastor, he was deeply influenced by the church and its role in the community. From a young age, he experienced racial segregation, which would later shape his commitment to fighting for equality and justice.

1948

In 1948, King graduated from Morehouse College, where he began to seriously consider his role in the world. Soon after, he entered the ministry, following in his father’s footsteps. During this time, he started developing the ideas about nonviolence and social justice that would define his leadership.

1955

King rose to national attention in 1955 when he helped lead the Montgomery Bus Boycott following the arrest of Rosa Parks. The protest lasted over a year and became a powerful example of nonviolent resistance. It ultimately led to a Supreme Court decision that ended segregation on public buses.

1957

By 1957, King had become a central figure in the Civil Rights Movement. He co-founded the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC), an organization dedicated to advancing civil rights through nonviolent protest. His leadership helped unify efforts across the South.

1963

In 1963, King played a major role in organizing the March on Washington, one of the largest political rallies in U.S. history. It was there that he delivered his iconic “I Have a Dream” speech, calling for an end to racism and envisioning a more equal future. This moment solidified his place as a global symbol of peace and justice.

1964

The following year, King was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for his commitment to nonviolent activism. At the time, he was one of the youngest recipients ever. The award recognized his leadership and the growing impact of the Civil Rights Movement.

1965

In 1965, King helped lead marches in Selma, Alabama, advocating for voting rights for African Americans. These demonstrations brought national attention to voter suppression and played a key role in the passage of the Voting Rights Act, a major legislative victory.

1968

On April 4, 1968, Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated in Memphis, Tennessee. His death shocked the nation, but his message and work continued to inspire future generations. Today, he is remembered as one of the most influential leaders in the fight for civil rights.
